Physics-Informed Neural Networks for Fluid Flow Simulation

Reconstructing the velocity and pressure fields of flow past a cylinder by embedding the Navier–Stokes equations directly into a neural network's training loss — and discovering the fluid's physical parameters from data alone.

PyTorch Navier–Stokes PINN (Raissi et al., 2019) Re = 100 cylinder wake 8 × 20 tanh MLP

01Motivation

Traditional numerical simulation of fluid flows — finite-volume or spectral solvers for the Navier–Stokes equations — is computationally intensive and time-consuming. Physics-informed neural networks (PINNs) offer an alternative: instead of learning purely from data, the network is told the universal laws that govern velocity and pressure fields. The PDE residual is added to the training loss, so the model learns dramatically faster and generalizes better than a purely data-driven approach, even from sparse, scattered measurements.

Physics as a constraint

The incompressible Navier–Stokes momentum equations are evaluated on the network's output via automatic differentiation and penalized as a residual loss.

Continuity by construction

The network predicts a stream function ψ; velocities are its derivatives (u = ∂ψ/∂y, v = −∂ψ/∂x), so mass conservation is satisfied exactly.

Parameter discovery

The convection and viscosity coefficients λ₁, λ₂ are unknown trainable scalars — the network recovers them from velocity data alone.

02Data & Setup

Training data comes from the openly published high-fidelity spectral DNS solution of 2D flow past a circular cylinder at Reynolds number 100 (the benchmark dataset of Raissi, Perdikaris & Karniadakis): a 100×50 spatial grid over the wake region, 200 snapshots in time. From the one million available space–time points, only 5,000 randomly scattered velocity measurements (≈0.5%) are used for training — the pressure field is never shown to the network and is recovered purely through the physics.

04Training Convergence

Combined loss (data misfit + Navier–Stokes residual) over Adam iterations followed by L-BFGS fine-tuning. Embedding the physics regularizes training: the loss drops smoothly and the PDE coefficients converge to their true values without ever being labeled.

05Going Further: Discovering the Equations Themselves

The model above assumes the Navier–Stokes form and fits two coefficients. This extension drops that assumption entirely: each momentum equation is expressed as a sparse combination of a 14-term candidate library — real physics terms (convection, pressure gradient, diffusion) mixed with decoys (u², u·v, v², lone velocities, a constant…). An L1 sparsity penalty forces the network to keep only the terms the data actually supports (SINDy-style sparse regression fused into the PINN loss, refined with STRidge-style sequential thresholding). The result: it rediscovers the structure of the Navier–Stokes equations from raw velocity samples — the true convection, pressure and diffusion terms emerge with the right signs and dominant magnitudes, while the decoy terms are driven an order of magnitude smaller.

06Method Summary

A fully-connected network (x, y, t) → (ψ, p) with 8 hidden layers of 20 tanh units is trained with the loss L = MSE(u, v) + MSE(f_u, f_v), where the momentum residuals f_u = u_t + λ₁(u u_x + v u_y) + p_x − λ₂(u_xx + u_yy) (and analogously f_v) are computed with automatic differentiation. Optimization runs 6,000 Adam steps followed by L-BFGS refinement. Because physical constraints are baked into training, the model learns faster and more data-efficiently than a purely data-driven network of the same size — and simultaneously solves an inverse problem, identifying the flow's convection and viscosity parameters.
